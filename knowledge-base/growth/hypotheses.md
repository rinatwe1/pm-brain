# Growth Hypotheses — Starting Points

## Common Hypotheses to Test
These are patterns observed across many products. Validate or invalidate for your specific context.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our biggest growth bottleneck is not acquisition — it's activation or retention
  Confidence: medium-high (activation is underinvested in relative to its impact in most products)
  Source: AARRR funnel analysis, Hacking Growth (Ellis & Brown)
  Test method: Quantify each AARRR stage with real numbers. For each stage, model the revenue impact of a 10% improvement. The stage with the highest modeled impact is your real bottleneck. If you haven't done this model, your current investment allocation is based on intuition rather than data.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: There is a specific early action that strongly predicts long-term retention
  Confidence: high (true for virtually every product — the aha moment exists, it's usually just unmeasured)
  Source: Aha moment concept, Hacking Growth (Ellis & Brown); Bowling Alley Framework, Product-Led Growth (Bush)
  Test method: Pull your cohort data. Segment Day-30 retained users and Day-30 churned users. Compare what each group did in their first session or first week. Look for actions that are significantly more common in the retained cohort. The action with the highest relative frequency difference is your candidate aha moment. Validate by building onboarding to funnel new users toward that action and measuring the retention impact.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our current go-to-market model (sales-led vs. product-led) is mismatched with our product's actual value delivery mechanism
  Confidence: medium (especially relevant for B2B products with high self-serve potential that rely on sales)
  Source: PLG model, Product-Led Growth (Bush)
  Test method: Apply the MOAT filter: Market (do users have budget authority, or does buying require committee approval?), Ocean conditions (is the space competitive enough that a PLG wedge creates differentiation?), Audience type (are end users and economic buyers the same person?), Time to value (can a user experience meaningful value within one session without human onboarding?). If three of the four factors favor PLG and you're running a sales-led motion, the mismatch is real.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Word-of-mouth is already happening organically and a formal referral program would amplify it significantly
  Confidence: medium (referral programs accelerate organic sharing but cannot create sharing that doesn't exist)
  Source: Referral amplification pattern, Hacking Growth (Ellis & Brown)
  Test method: Survey a sample of new users: "How did you hear about us?" If fewer than 15% say "from a friend or colleague," the organic referral signal is too weak for a program to amplify — the product itself needs work first. If 20% or more are already arriving through word of mouth, a referral program can be a high-leverage growth investment.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Reducing the time from signup to first value would meaningfully improve our activation rate
  Confidence: high (almost universally true — the longest path to first value almost always has unnecessary steps)
  Source: Straight-line onboarding principle, Product-Led Growth (Bush)
  Test method: Map every step in your current new-user experience from account creation to first meaningful outcome. Measure drop-off at each step. Time the median new user's journey. Then list every step that is not strictly necessary to reach first value and estimate the activation impact of removing each one. Build the case for a simplified onboarding experiment before shipping it.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our paid acquisition channels have a positive ROI at our current stage
  Confidence: low for pre-PMF products; higher for post-PMF products with measured LTV
  Source: Acquisition efficiency principle, Hacking Growth (Ellis & Brown)
  Test method: Calculate Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) from each paid channel. Compare to measured Lifetime Value (LTV) for acquired cohorts. LTV requires real retention data — not a projection. The ratio must be positive (LTV > CAC) and the payback period must be within your runway. If you don't have real LTV data yet, paid acquisition is a bet on an unmeasured assumption.
