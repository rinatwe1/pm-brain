# Growth Knowledge Base
## Pre-loaded Frameworks

---

### The High-Tempo Testing Process — Hacking Growth (Sean Ellis & Morgan Brown)
**What it is:** A structured weekly cycle for running growth experiments across any stage of the funnel. The cycle has four phases: analyze the available data to identify the highest-leverage area, generate ideas for experiments in that area, score and prioritize ideas by expected impact and implementation cost, and run the top-priority experiments. The team reviews results at a fixed weekly cadence and uses what was learned to drive the next cycle.
**Core insight:** The primary competitive differentiator between high-growth companies and slow-growth companies is not which experiments they run — it's how many experiments per unit of time they run. The compounding of small wins from a high-tempo testing process outperforms any single "big bet" growth initiative. The discipline of the process — structured scoring, fixed review cadence, data-driven prioritization — is what creates compounding results over time.
**PM use case:** Use to establish a growth team operating rhythm. The scoring system (impact × confidence ÷ effort) is the key artifact — it makes prioritization defensible and reduces the influence of the highest-paid person's opinion on which experiments to run.

### Product-Led Growth — Product-Led Growth (Wes Bush)
**What it is:** A go-to-market strategy where the product itself generates acquisition, activation, and retention without relying primarily on a sales team or traditional marketing. In PLG, users discover the product, experience its value directly (often through a free tier or trial), and self-select into paid plans when they're ready. The sales team, if it exists, focuses on expanding accounts that have already demonstrated engagement rather than generating initial interest.
**Core insight:** PLG is not just a pricing decision — it's an organizational orientation. It requires the product to deliver value quickly enough that users will spread it to colleagues and convert without a sales conversation. The implication is that every friction point in the onboarding and activation experience is a sales problem, not just a UX problem. PLG organizations treat onboarding conversion with the same urgency that traditional sales organizations treat pipeline conversion.
**PM use case:** Use when evaluating go-to-market approach for a new product or evaluating why conversion from trial to paid is underperforming. The central question is: can a user experience the core value of the product without talking to anyone at your company? If not, that's either a fundamental PLG blocker or a significant onboarding improvement opportunity.

### The Bowling Alley Framework — Product-Led Growth (Wes Bush)
**What it is:** A model for structuring the new-user experience around two elements: a straight-line path (the minimum sequence of actions that leads to first value) and bumpers (mechanisms that redirect users who are drifting off that path). Borrowed from the metaphor of bumper bowling, where the bumpers prevent gutter balls but don't force the ball — they just keep it in the game.
**Core insight:** New users who drift off the straight line almost never find their way back to it on their own. They don't fail loudly — they quietly disengage. The combination of a clearly defined straight line (which requires PMs to actually define what "first value" means, which many haven't) and active bumpers (in-app guidance, triggered emails, success milestones) is what turns "interesting product" into "product people use more than once."
**PM use case:** Use to audit your current onboarding flow. First, define the straight line: what is the single sequence of actions a new user must complete to experience core value? Then map your current onboarding against that line. Every step that isn't on the straight line is either a detour (remove it) or a bumper (position it differently). Finally, identify where users currently fall off the line and add a bumper at that point.

### The AARRR Funnel — Hacking Growth (Ellis & Brown, popularized by Dave McClure)
**What it is:** A five-stage framework for categorizing growth metrics and identifying where the biggest leverage exists: Acquisition (how users discover the product), Activation (the first experience that demonstrates value), Retention (whether users return), Referral (whether users recommend to others), and Revenue (whether engagement converts to economic value).
**Core insight:** The framework's primary value is diagnostic. Most organizations intuitively know what their acquisition numbers look like, but many have never calculated activation or retention rates with the same rigor. When you quantify all five stages and model the impact of improvement at each, the highest-leverage stage is almost always not the one receiving the most investment. Activation is chronically underinvested. Referral is often ignored entirely until the product is already growing. Revenue optimization frequently gets deferred indefinitely.
**PM use case:** Use quarterly to audit where growth investment is being made versus where it would have the highest impact. Build a simple model: for each stage, calculate current conversion, and estimate the revenue impact of a 10% improvement. The resulting ranking almost always reveals an allocation mismatch that makes for a compelling prioritization conversation.

### The Must-Have Experience — Hacking Growth (Ellis & Brown)
**What it is:** The principle that growth investment should only begin after the product has been verified to deliver an experience that a meaningful segment of users would genuinely miss if it disappeared. The must-have threshold is operationalized through the PMF survey: 40% or more of active users responding "very disappointed" to the question of what they'd feel if the product were gone.
**Core insight:** The "must-have" framing shifts the question from "do users like this?" (almost always yes) to "do users depend on this?" (much harder to achieve). Dependence is what drives retention, referral, and willingness to pay. After reaching the 40% threshold, the strategic move is to study the "very disappointed" cohort intensively: what job are they hiring the product for? What made them fall in love? What would they use instead? The answers define the growth strategy — who to acquire more of, and what experience to make more users feel.
**PM use case:** Run the survey before any growth investment decision. Also use it post-launch to identify whether the product has evolved to must-have status or is still in the "nice to have" zone. When the must-have score is below threshold, the next priority is not a growth experiment — it's understanding what would need to be true for the score to cross 40%.
