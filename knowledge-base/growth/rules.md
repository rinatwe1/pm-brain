# Growth Rules — Apply by Default

## Confirmed Rules
These are validated across many products and teams. Apply unless you have contradicting evidence.

---

### Rule: Validate the must-have experience before activating any growth channel
**Source:** Hacking Growth — Sean Ellis & Morgan Brown
**The principle:** Growth tactics work by amplifying what's already there. If the product experience isn't genuinely valuable yet, scaling distribution only delivers more users to a product they won't stick with.
**Why it works:** A referral program, a viral loop, or a paid acquisition channel can meaningfully accelerate a product that customers already love. Applied before that condition is met, those same mechanisms produce short-term user spikes that evaporate, deplete budget, and create organizational pressure to "grow our way out" of a product problem — which never works. The most efficient path to sustainable growth is to earn it by solving a real problem well.
**How to apply:** Before initiating growth experiments, run a must-have assessment: survey active users, measure Day 30 retention, look for organic referral signals (how many new users heard about you from someone they know?). Growth investment requires at least two of these signals to be healthy.
**Exception:** Network-effect products and marketplace products sometimes need a minimum scale before the must-have experience is even possible to assess — the product isn't valuable until there are enough participants. In these cases, limited acquisition to reach minimum viable density is acceptable, but it should be framed as infrastructure investment, not growth.

### Rule: Structure growth as a cross-functional team, not a marketing channel
**Source:** Hacking Growth — Sean Ellis & Morgan Brown
**The principle:** Effective growth requires engineering, data analysis, and marketing to work together in a tight weekly loop. When these functions operate in separate organizational silos, growth experiments move slowly, data insights arrive too late to act on, and engineering bandwidth for growth is perpetually deprioritized.
**Why it works:** Growth experiments live at the intersection of all three disciplines. A marketing insight that can't be implemented without a three-sprint engineering queue is a dead idea. A data pattern that nobody with product context is analyzing generates reports nobody acts on. The organizational structure determines the speed of the feedback loop, and speed of the feedback loop is the primary predictor of growth team effectiveness.
**How to apply:** Evaluate whether growth work is genuinely cross-functional or whether "growth" is a marketing initiative that requests engineering help when convenient. The test: do engineers, data analysts, and marketers meet weekly to review experiment results and decide what to run next? If not, the team isn't structured for growth.
**Exception:** Very early-stage companies where the founding team plays all roles simultaneously. As soon as there is functional specialization, intentional cross-functional collaboration on growth should be built in from the start.

### Rule: Reduce onboarding to the minimum path to first value
**Source:** Product-Led Growth — Wes Bush
**The principle:** New users arrive with zero familiarity, zero patience, and zero commitment. The only thing that converts them into retained users is experiencing the core value of the product as quickly as possible. Every step, screen, and question that delays that moment is a leak in the activation funnel.
**Why it works:** Users don't abandon products because the feature set is too small — they abandon them because they couldn't figure out how to get value from the feature set that exists. Onboarding friction is almost always invisible to product teams because the people who built the product already understand it. Mapping the first-time experience explicitly and measuring drop-off at each step makes the invisible visible.
**How to apply:** Map the complete new-user journey from signup to first meaningful outcome. For every step that is not directly necessary to reach that outcome, ask: "What would break if we removed this?" Steps that survive that test stay. Steps that don't get removed or deferred to after first value is delivered.
**Exception:** Products in regulated domains (healthcare, finance, security) where compliance or trust requirements genuinely must come before value can be delivered. Even in those cases, distinguish between the legally required steps and the "nice to have" profile-building steps — and remove the latter.

### Rule: Invest deliberately across all five growth levers, not just acquisition
**Source:** Hacking Growth — Sean Ellis & Morgan Brown
**The principle:** The typical organizational default is to allocate most of the growth budget to top-of-funnel acquisition while systematically underinvesting in activation, retention, referral, and revenue optimization. This is almost always the wrong allocation.
**Why it works:** Improving retention has an outsized compounding effect on all other metrics. A 5% improvement in monthly retention compounds into dramatically higher LTV over 12 months. Improving activation multiplies the ROI of every acquisition dollar spent — more of the users you paid for actually become engaged users. Improving referral reduces effective CAC. Teams that analyze their funnel holistically almost always find that the highest-leverage investment is not at the top.
**How to apply:** Quantify each lever in your AARRR funnel with actual numbers. Calculate the impact of a 10% improvement at each stage on 12-month revenue or active users. Use this to rank where to invest. Revisit the analysis quarterly as the funnel metrics change.
**Exception:** True launch-phase products with zero users — some minimum acquisition is necessary before you have enough users to analyze any other lever. But even in launch phase, track onboarding data from day one so you can optimize activation as soon as there's a sample size.
