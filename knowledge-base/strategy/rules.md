# Strategy Rules — Apply by Default

## Confirmed Rules
These are validated across many products and teams. Apply unless you have contradicting evidence.

---

### Rule: Hold teams accountable to outcomes, not to outputs
**Source:** Escaping the Build Trap — Melissa Perri; Continuous Discovery Habits — Teresa Torres
**The principle:** A feature shipped is not value created. The only thing that counts is whether customer behavior or a business metric actually changed. Teams rewarded for shipping will optimize for shipping.
**Why it works:** When the measure of progress is output — velocity, story points, features released — teams naturally optimize for that measure. They move faster, cut corners on discovery, and ship things that don't move the needle. The organizational reward structure determines the product's direction more than any process or framework. Switching accountability to outcomes forces a different conversation: not "did we ship it?" but "did it work?"
**How to apply:** Replace output metrics in team reviews and stakeholder updates with outcome metrics. Ask "what changed for customers or the business?" after every release. If you can't answer, that's the finding.
**Exception:** In a pre-PMF startup phase, raw output speed can be a reasonable secondary proxy — maximizing experiments per unit of time is legitimate. But even then, the criterion for a "good" experiment is whether it generated validated learning, not whether it was shipped on time.

### Rule: Let the vision anchor while the strategy adapts
**Source:** Strategize — Roman Pichler; The Lean Startup — Eric Ries
**The principle:** The "why" of a product (the vision) should be durable enough to survive multiple rounds of strategic pivots. The "how" (strategy) should be flexible enough to change when the evidence demands it.
**Why it works:** Conflating vision with strategy creates two failure modes. When teams treat the vision as flexible, they lose coherence — the product drifts and teams can't make consistent decisions. When teams treat strategy as fixed, they refuse to adapt when the market gives them feedback that the approach isn't working. The discipline is knowing which level you're operating at and how much stability it requires.
**How to apply:** When you're considering a major change, name what level it operates at. Is this a change to the vision (who benefits, and how fundamentally)? Or a change to strategy (which market, which value proposition, which business model)? Vision changes require much more deliberation and stakeholder alignment than strategy pivots.
**Exception:** If reality proves the original vision was built on a false assumption — a market that doesn't exist, a technology that isn't feasible, a user need that wasn't real — the right move is to revisit the vision rather than forcing strategy to serve a broken foundation.

### Rule: Prove the product is loved before scaling anything
**Source:** INSPIRED — Marty Cagan; The Lean Startup — Eric Ries
**The principle:** Pouring resources into acquisition, sales, or marketing before achieving product/market fit doesn't accelerate success — it accelerates the rate of burning money on something that isn't working yet.
**Why it works:** Scale amplifies whatever you already have. If you scale a product that customers don't genuinely love, you acquire more customers who churn, run more ads that don't convert, hire more salespeople who can't close, and burn through runway faster. Every dollar and every day spent scaling before PMF is a bet that the product is already right — and that bet is usually wrong.
**How to apply:** Define explicit PMF criteria before the scaling conversation begins. The minimum bar is 40%+ of active users saying they'd be "very disappointed" if the product disappeared. Supplement with retention data (do users come back?) and organic growth signals (are users telling others?). Scale when you have two or three of these signals, not one.
**Exception:** Network-effect products and some enterprise products need a minimum viable scale to generate the data required to measure PMF in the first place. In these cases, controlled acquisition for learning purposes is acceptable — but it must be explicitly framed as a research investment, not as growth.

### Rule: Business cases for unproven ideas are fiction — use assumption testing instead
**Source:** INSPIRED — Marty Cagan
**The principle:** The two inputs every business case requires — how much will it make, and how much will it cost — cannot be known for genuinely novel product ideas before you've tested them. Precision in a business case for a new product creates false confidence, not insight.
**Why it works:** Organizations that require rigorous business cases before investing in new ideas systematically under-invest in innovation. The business case becomes a political document — whoever can construct the most compelling spreadsheet wins resources, regardless of whether the underlying assumptions are grounded. Meanwhile, the genuinely uncertain and novel ideas (which are the ones worth betting on) can't produce a credible business case and never get funded.
**How to apply:** Replace "write a business case" with "identify the riskiest assumptions and design the cheapest test to validate them." Invest in learning, not in planning. For existing product improvements with known customers and established data, traditional business cases work fine.
**Exception:** Incremental improvements to existing, well-understood products where reliable historical data exists. The further an initiative is from the existing core, the less useful a traditional business case becomes.

### Rule: Strategy is defined as much by what you refuse to do as by what you commit to
**Source:** INSPIRED — Marty Cagan; Lean Product Playbook — Dan Olsen
**The principle:** A strategy that tries to serve everyone, solve every problem, and enter every market is not a strategy — it's a wish list. Real strategic value comes from the clarity created by deliberate exclusion.
**Why it works:** Resource allocation only creates focus when there are more compelling options than resources to pursue them all. If you say yes to everything, teams get pulled in every direction, progress slows to a crawl, and the product ends up mediocre in every area rather than excellent in one. Saying no to a genuinely good idea is the hardest and most important strategic skill.
**How to apply:** When evaluating new opportunities, apply the strategy as a filter. Ask: does this serve our target customer? Does it reinforce our core value proposition? Does it fit our business model? If three yeses, explore it. If any no, archive it with the reasoning — don't just reject it, document why.
**Exception:** Early-stage pre-strategy exploration, where generating a wide range of possibilities is the right move before narrowing. The strategy emerges from exploring many options, not from applying a filter that doesn't exist yet.
