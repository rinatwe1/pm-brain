# Strategy Hypotheses — Starting Points

## Common Hypotheses to Test
These are patterns observed across many products. Validate or invalidate for your specific context.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our team is in the build trap — we measure success by what we shipped, not by what changed
  Confidence: medium (true for the majority of product teams in large organizations)
  Source: Build Trap pattern, Escaping the Build Trap (Perri)
  Test method: Pull your last five shipped features. For each one, state (a) what outcome you expected, (b) what metric you used to track it, and (c) what actually happened. If you can't answer (b) or (c) for most of them, the build trap is the operating model.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our product vision is not shared — different people would describe it differently
  Confidence: low (shared vision is rare and requires deliberate maintenance)
  Source: Vision stability principle, Strategize (Pichler)
  Test method: Ask five people from different functions to write down the product vision in one or two sentences without collaborating. Compare. If the answers diverge significantly, there is no shared vision — there are competing assumptions masquerading as alignment.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: We have not yet achieved product/market fit
  Confidence: varies by product age and growth signals
  Source: PMF milestone model, INSPIRED (Cagan); Must-Have Score, Hacking Growth (Ellis)
  Test method: Survey a random sample of active users with the question: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" Tally the percentage who answer "very disappointed." Below 40%, focus on understanding the users who would be very disappointed — who they are and why — before scaling anything else.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our product strategy is not actually constraining our roadmap decisions
  Confidence: high (most roadmaps contain items that don't trace to any stated strategy)
  Source: Strategy-roadmap alignment, Escaping the Build Trap (Perri)
  Test method: Take each item currently on the roadmap and ask "which element of our stated strategy does this advance?" Items that can't be traced to a strategic goal are tactical noise. If more than a third of the roadmap fails this test, the strategy isn't real — it's a document that doesn't govern decisions.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our business strategy and product strategy are misaligned — the product is heading in a different direction than the business
  Confidence: medium (especially common after reorgs, leadership changes, or market pivots)
  Source: Strategy hierarchy, Strategize (Pichler)
  Test method: State your business strategy and product strategy each in one sentence. Present both to a skeptical colleague who knows both sides of the business. Ask them to identify the gap or the contradiction. If they find one immediately, it's real and probably well-known but unaddressed.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: We are applying core-innovation management practices to transformational initiatives — and killing them in the process
  Confidence: medium (common in mature organizations attempting to innovate)
  Source: Innovation Ambition Matrix, Strategize (Pichler, Nagji & Tuff)
  Test method: Identify any initiative your team considers "transformational" or "experimental." Then list the management practices applied to it: Is it required to produce a business case? Is it governed by quarterly OKRs? Is it evaluated against revenue targets? If yes to two or more, it's being managed as core work — and transformational work managed as core work almost always loses.
