# Strategy Knowledge Base
## Pre-loaded Frameworks

---

### The Build Trap — Escaping the Build Trap (Melissa Perri)
**What it is:** A systemic organizational failure mode where companies measure product success by the volume of features shipped rather than by the value those features create. The build trap is self-reinforcing: when teams are rewarded for shipping, they optimize for shipping, which generates more output but not more outcomes, which leads to calls for even more output.
**Core insight:** The trap is not primarily a product problem — it's a management and incentive problem. A PM who understands outcomes but works in an organization that rewards output will eventually conform to the incentive structure or leave. Breaking the trap requires changing what leadership asks about, what gets celebrated, and how performance is evaluated — all the way up to the executive level.
**PM use case:** Use as a diagnostic framework when a team feels busy but not impactful. The test is simple: take your last five shipped features and find out what metric each one moved. If you can't answer, you're in the trap. Use it in retrospectives and strategy reviews to surface the pattern, and use it to make the case for outcome-based OKRs.

### The Product Kata — Escaping the Build Trap (Melissa Perri)
**What it is:** A four-step iterative improvement loop adapted from Toyota's manufacturing improvement practice. Applied to product work, it runs as: (1) clarify the desired direction — what outcome are we trying to achieve? (2) understand the current condition — where are we starting from? (3) define the next target — what's the smallest measurable step toward the goal? (4) experiment toward the target — what's the cheapest way to test whether we're on the right track?
**Core insight:** The kata is a structured replacement for roadmap-driven development. Instead of committing to a feature in a quarterly plan, a team runs the kata continuously: understand the goal, assess the gap, design an experiment, measure, repeat. It turns "here's our plan" into "here's our hypothesis and how we're testing it" — a much more honest and effective way to work.
**PM use case:** Use when a team is struggling to connect their day-to-day work to a meaningful goal, or when stakeholders keep asking for specific features rather than outcomes. The kata gives structure to the hypothesis-testing conversation and makes the learning process legible to non-practitioners.

### Vision → Strategy → Roadmap — Strategize (Roman Pichler)
**What it is:** A cascading hierarchy of product direction, where each level is more specific and more subject to change than the one above it. Vision captures the purpose and end state (durable, inspiring, broad). Strategy maps how the vision will be realized for which customers through which market and business model (changes when the approach isn't working). Roadmap translates strategy into sequenced releases with goals (changes frequently as you learn). Tactics cover the details of execution (always in flux).
**Core insight:** Most product dysfunction can be traced to confusion between levels. Teams debate tactical details when they haven't resolved strategic alignment. Roadmaps get changed because the vision wasn't shared. A strategy exists on paper but doesn't actually constrain roadmap decisions. The hierarchy makes the levels explicit and forces conversations at the right altitude.
**PM use case:** Use as a conversation structure with leadership and stakeholders. When someone proposes a feature, ask which level the conversation is at: does this reflect a new strategic direction, or is it a tactical execution choice within an agreed strategy? When roadmap items don't connect to any stated strategic goal, use the hierarchy to expose the gap.

### The Innovation Ambition Matrix — Strategize (Pichler, citing Nagji & Tuff)
**What it is:** A three-zone framework for categorizing product investments by risk and distance from current capabilities. Core innovation involves optimizing an existing product for the existing customer base — low risk, predictable returns, manageable with operational discipline. Adjacent innovation means taking existing strengths into new markets or creating new products for existing customers — medium risk, requires experimentation. Transformational innovation addresses entirely new markets with entirely new offerings — high risk, high potential, requires a different management approach.
**Core insight:** The mistake most mature organizations make is applying the same management approach to all three zones. Transformational innovation cannot be managed with business cases, milestone reviews, and quarterly OKRs — these tools kill it. It requires a different governance model: more tolerance for ambiguity, longer time horizons, and the explicit acceptance that most experiments will fail. Mixing the approaches produces the worst of both: transformational ideas killed by core-zone management, and core business under-managed because leadership is distracted by moonshots.
**PM use case:** Use when advocating for different resource allocation or governance approaches for different types of work. If a transformational initiative is being judged by core-innovation standards (ROI projections, fixed timelines), use this framework to name the problem.

### Product/Market Fit as the Primary Milestone — INSPIRED (Marty Cagan)
**What it is:** The recognition that before PMF, a startup's only real job is to find PMF — not to scale, not to optimize, not to perfect. PMF is the moment when a product serves a customer need so well that a sustainable, growing customer base emerges organically.
**Core insight:** The bottleneck for most product failures is not engineering or design — it's the quality of product discovery. The teams that reach PMF are not necessarily the best engineers or the best designers; they are the best at figuring out what to build. Engineering excellence applied to the wrong product is not a path to success. The investment in discovery is not optional.
**PM use case:** Use when scoping the work for a new product initiative or a startup. The question "are we allocating enough time and resources to discovery?" should be asked explicitly before any build sprint begins. If discovery is being compressed to accelerate delivery, name the risk.

### Build-Measure-Learn — The Lean Startup (Eric Ries)
**What it is:** A framework for treating every product initiative as an experiment. The cycle is: formulate an idea as a testable hypothesis, build the minimum thing needed to test it, measure customer response to generate data, and learn whether to continue, adjust, or abandon. Then repeat, faster.
**Core insight:** The constraint is not the cycle itself — every team goes through some version of build-measure-learn. The constraint is the speed of the loop. A team that runs the cycle in two weeks learns at six times the rate of a team that runs it quarterly. The competitive advantage of fast-moving companies is not that they have better ideas — it's that they test more of them per unit of time and therefore kill bad ideas earlier and double down on good ones faster.
**PM use case:** Use to frame investment conversations with leadership: what is the minimum investment needed to learn whether this idea is worth pursuing? Use it to defend small-scope experiments against pressure to build the "full thing" before learning whether the thing is worth building at all.
