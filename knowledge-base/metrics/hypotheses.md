# Metrics Hypotheses — Starting Points

## Common Hypotheses to Test
These are patterns observed across many products. Validate or invalidate for your specific context.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our current key results measure outputs rather than outcomes
  Confidence: high (the majority of teams write output KRs initially)
  Source: Output vs. outcome distinction, OKRs Done Right (Gilad)
  Test method: For each key result in your current OKR set, apply the test: "Can my team complete this KR without any customer behavior changing?" If yes, it's an output KR. Count what fraction of your KRs fail this test. If more than half fail, your OKR practice needs a rewrite before the next cycle.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our OKRs are functioning as performance contracts rather than learning tools, causing teams to sandbagg targets
  Confidence: medium (common when OKRs are tied to reviews or when leadership reacts badly to misses)
  Source: OKR incentive corruption pattern, OKRs Done Right (Gilad)
  Test method: Ask team members privately: "What would happen if we missed our OKRs this quarter?" If the answer involves career risk or manager disappointment, the OKRs are functioning as performance contracts. Cross-reference by looking at the ambition level of targets — are they achievable at 100% rather than requiring real stretch to hit 70%?

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: We are tracking metrics that can improve while the product gets worse (vanity metrics)
  Confidence: medium (especially common in early-stage products measuring total signups or page views)
  Source: Vanity metrics concept, The Lean Startup (Ries); Hacking Growth (Ellis)
  Test method: For each metric in your reporting dashboard, ask: "Could this metric go up in a week where product quality significantly declined?" Total registered users can rise while retention falls. App downloads can increase while activation rate drops. If the answer is yes, the metric is a vanity metric — useful for context, not for decisions.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our product has not been tested for product/market fit with a real measurement method
  Confidence: varies — many teams assume PMF based on qualitative enthusiasm rather than measurement
  Source: PMF survey method, Hacking Growth (Ellis)
  Test method: If you haven't run a structured PMF survey with a representative sample of active users, your PMF assessment is untested. Run the survey. Segment the results by user segment, tenure, and use case. PMF may be true for a subset of your users but not the full population — which is itself a critical strategic finding.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Retention is the weakest metric in our growth model, and fixing it would have a larger impact than improving acquisition
  Confidence: high (retention is underinvested in relative to acquisition in most organizations)
  Source: Retention-first principle, Lean Product Playbook (Olsen)
  Test method: Pull retention cohort data for the last three months. Calculate Day 1, Day 7, and Day 30 retention. Find category benchmarks (consumer mobile, B2B SaaS, etc.) and compare. If your retention is below the median for your category, every dollar spent on acquisition is running a deficit — fix retention first.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our quarterly OKR cycle length is mismatched with the actual feedback loop of our product
  Confidence: medium (depends heavily on the product's natural cadence and metric responsiveness)
  Source: Cycle-length fit principle, OKRs Done Right (Gilad)
  Test method: Ask: how long does it realistically take to run one iteration of your core experiment — from idea to measurable outcome? If the answer is two weeks, quarterly OKRs are too slow and teams lose the connection between actions and results. If the answer is six months, quarterly OKRs create false signals of progress. The cycle length should match the feedback loop.
