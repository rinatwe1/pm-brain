# Metrics Rules — Apply by Default

## Confirmed Rules
These are validated across many products and teams. Apply unless you have contradicting evidence.

---

### Rule: Key results must measure what changed, not what was done
**Source:** OKRs Done Right — Itamar Gilad
**The principle:** A key result that tracks activities or deliverables tells you whether your team was busy. A key result that tracks behavioral change in users or movement in business metrics tells you whether your work mattered.
**Why it works:** Output key results contain a hidden assumption: that doing the activity will produce the desired impact. That assumption is almost always untested. When teams commit to "launch three features" as a key result, they're pre-committing to a solution before validating whether that solution achieves anything. Outcome key results keep the real goal visible and force honest evaluation of whether the work is actually working.
**How to apply:** For each key result in your current OKRs, ask: "Can I complete this KR without any customer behavior changing?" If yes, it's an output KR. Rewrite it as a behavior or metric change: instead of "ship the onboarding redesign," write "increase day-7 activation rate from 34% to 50%."
**Exception:** Infrastructure, compliance, and foundational technical work genuinely cannot be expressed as customer behavioral outcomes. For these, milestone KRs are acceptable — but flag them explicitly as proxies and add a note on what outcome the milestone is intended to enable.

### Rule: Keep OKR evaluation entirely separate from performance evaluation
**Source:** OKRs Done Right — Itamar Gilad
**The principle:** The moment OKRs are tied to bonuses, promotions, or formal performance reviews, the measurement system is corrupted. Teams will set targets they can guarantee, hide problems, and report progress that looks better than it is.
**Why it works:** OKRs work when they're honest instruments for learning and alignment. They require teams to set ambitious targets (where 70% achievement is the expected success signal) and to report honestly when things aren't working. All of this requires psychological safety. Tying OKRs to compensation destroys psychological safety: the incentive to look good overrides the incentive to learn. Teams converge on sandbagged targets and inflated reporting.
**How to apply:** Create explicit separation in your organization's language and process. OKR reviews are learning conversations about product effectiveness. Performance reviews are separate conversations about individual contribution, judgment, and growth. When anyone conflates the two, name the distinction.
**Exception:** There are no meaningful exceptions to this rule. The structure of the incentive determines the behavior more reliably than any coaching or culture initiative.

### Rule: Fewer OKRs produce more focus and better results than many OKRs
**Source:** OKRs Done Right — Itamar Gilad
**The principle:** A team with six active objectives is not more ambitious than a team with two — it is less focused, and will achieve less across all six than a focused team achieves across two.
**Why it works:** OKRs only create focus if they require choosing between competing priorities. A team with too many objectives never has to make that choice — everything is a priority, which means nothing is. The result is effort distributed thinly across all objectives, slow progress everywhere, and the inability to identify which investments actually drove results.
**How to apply:** The practical limit for a product team is three objectives with three key results each, maximum. Most high-performing teams operate with two objectives. Paring down requires explicitly prioritizing and explicitly deprioritizing — which is the hardest and most valuable strategic skill.
**Exception:** Large portfolio-level OKRs spanning many independent teams can have more total items, since each team has its own sub-set. The fewer-is-better principle applies at the team level, not the portfolio summary level.

### Rule: Retention must be solved before acquisition is scaled
**Source:** Lean Product Playbook — Dan Olsen; Hacking Growth — Ellis & Brown
**The principle:** Spending on user acquisition before retention is acceptable is the equivalent of filling a bucket with a hole in the bottom. The hole must be fixed first.
**Why it works:** The math is unforgiving. If monthly retention is 60%, then 40% of every user you acquire is gone within a month. Every dollar spent acquiring those users is wasted, and the product never reaches the scale needed to generate defensible data. The only metrics that matter for the retention question are: do users come back, how often, and for how long? If the answer to any of these is "not enough," acquisition spend is premature.
**How to apply:** Before approving any meaningful acquisition budget, run a retention analysis: Day 1, Day 7, and Day 30 retention rates. Compare to benchmarks for your category (consumer apps, B2B SaaS, etc.). If retention is below category median, the acquisition conversation should not happen.
**Exception:** When your product has a long natural usage cycle (seasonal tools, annual recurring tasks), standard retention metrics don't apply directly. In these cases, define the appropriate return-signal for your product's natural cadence before running the retention analysis.
