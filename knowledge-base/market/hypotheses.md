# Market Hypotheses — Starting Points

## Common Hypotheses to Test
These are patterns observed across many products. Validate/invalidate for your specific context.

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our main competitors are direct substitutes, not just alternative approaches to the same job
  Confidence: medium
  Source: Competing Against Luck (Christensen)
  Test method: Ask churned users what they switched to, and why. If most answers are not other products in your category, your competitive frame is wrong.

- [ ] Hypothesis: There is meaningful differentiation in our market that customers can articulate
  Confidence: medium
  Source: Obviously Awesome (Appcues), Positioning (Ries & Trout)
  Test method: Ask 10 prospects to describe the difference between you and your top competitor. If answers are vague or inconsistent, differentiation is not landing.

- [ ] Hypothesis: The market we're targeting is growing, not flat or contracting
  Confidence: varies
  Source: The Lean Startup (Ries)
  Test method: Find 3 independent signals of market growth (search trends, VC investment in the category, customer job frequency). Flat or declining trends are a strategic risk worth documenting.

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our primary segment is homogeneous enough to serve with one core product
  Confidence: medium
  Source: Crossing the Chasm (Moore)
  Test method: Compare the top 10 customer use cases. If they require materially different features, you may have two segments packaged as one — which dilutes focus.

- [ ] Hypothesis: We are not stuck in the chasm — we have transitioned from early adopters to mainstream buyers
  Confidence: varies
  Source: Crossing the Chasm (Moore)
  Test method: Count the ratio of reference customers to total customers. If almost all buyers require a personal recommendation or a case study to close, you may still be chasm-crossing.

- [ ] Hypothesis: There is a network effect in our product that will compound as we scale
  Confidence: low (most products overestimate this)
  Source: Platform Revolution (Parker et al.)
  Test method: Define exactly which user action creates value for another user, and measure whether that value is increasing. If you cannot measure it, you don't have a network effect yet.
