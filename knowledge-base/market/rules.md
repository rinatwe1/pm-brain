# Market Rules

## Rule: Update competitor map quarterly
Source: Competitive Intelligence best practices
The principle: Markets move. A competitive map older than 90 days is likely wrong in at least one material way.
Why it works: Competitors ship, pivot, get funded, and disappear. Stale competitive knowledge leads to wrong positioning decisions.
How to apply: Set a calendar reminder. Review competitor websites, changelogs, pricing, and recent funding announcements. Update .pm-brain/knowledge/market/ after each review.
Exception: In rapidly shifting categories (AI tools, crypto), monthly updates may be necessary.

## Rule: Validate your competitive frame with lost deals, not just won ones
Source: The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick), Competing Against Luck (Christensen)
The principle: The competitors you fear and the alternatives customers actually use are often different lists.
Why it works: Customers who chose you will rationalize your strengths. Customers who rejected you will tell you what you're actually competing against.
How to apply: Interview every churned customer and every lost prospect. Ask "what did you end up using instead, and why?" The answer maps your real competitive landscape.
Exception: Early-stage products may not have enough churn data — use discovery interviews to ask about current alternatives instead.

## Rule: Know which jobs you are not doing
Source: Jobs to Be Done (Ulwick), Competing Against Luck (Christensen)
The principle: Scope decisions are also market positioning decisions. What you refuse to do defines your category as much as what you do.
Why it works: Products that try to do everything for everyone are easily displaced by focused alternatives that do one thing better.
How to apply: Maintain an explicit list of jobs you have decided not to serve. Revisit quarterly to check whether any have become strategic.
Exception: Platform businesses may deliberately expand their job coverage over time — but each expansion should be a deliberate bet, not scope creep.

## Rule: Your positioning is what you say consistently, not what you mean internally
Source: Positioning (Ries & Trout), Obviously Awesome (Appcues)
The principle: Positioning is a perception battle, not a fact battle. What customers believe about your product is your market position, regardless of what your internal team believes.
Why it works: Inconsistent messaging fragments market perception and makes it hard for any one message to stick. Customers repeat what they hear consistently.
How to apply: Write a one-sentence positioning statement. Evaluate every piece of external communication against it. If something contradicts the sentence, decide which to change.
Exception: Category creation may require intentionally disrupting an established frame — but this requires consistency in the new frame, not inconsistency overall.

## Rule: Chasm-crossing requires a complete solution for a narrow beachhead
Source: Crossing the Chasm (Moore)
The principle: Mainstream buyers need a whole product — the core feature plus all the supporting elements that make it work for their use case.
Why it works: Early adopters tolerate rough edges because they buy vision. Mainstream buyers do not — they need reliability, integrations, and support that make the product complete.
How to apply: Pick the narrowest beachhead segment. List every element of a whole product for that segment. Systematically close each gap before expanding.
Exception: Some B2C consumer products can cross the chasm through virality without a whole product strategy — but this is the exception, not the rule.
