# Discovery Rules — Apply by Default

## Confirmed Rules
These are validated across many products and teams. Apply unless you have contradicting evidence.

---

### Rule: Ground every research question in the past, not the future
**Source:** The Mom Test — Rob Fitzpatrick
**The principle:** Questions about what someone *would* do or *might* buy produce optimistic fiction. Questions about what someone *actually did* last week produce evidence.
**Why it works:** People answer hypothetical questions based on how they want to be seen, not based on how they actually behave. Asking "how do you currently handle this?" bypasses social desirability bias and forces real recall of real events — including the workarounds, frustrations, and tools that reveal true pain level.
**How to apply:** Before any research session, convert your hypothetical questions into past-behavior questions. "Would you pay for X?" becomes "What have you already tried to solve this?" "Do you care about Y?" becomes "When's the last time Y caused you a problem — walk me through it."
**Exception:** Genuinely novel categories where no past behavior exists yet (e.g., an entirely new technology). Even then, anchor in adjacent experiences — what did they do when facing the closest analogous situation?

### Rule: If they haven't already tried to solve it, it's not a real problem
**Source:** The Mom Test — Rob Fitzpatrick
**The principle:** Active solution-seeking is the most reliable leading indicator of a problem worth solving. Stated interest without prior action almost never converts to paying behavior.
**Why it works:** Pain that's strong enough to drive a purchase decision first drives search behavior. Someone who says "I'd definitely pay for that" but has never searched, asked a colleague, bought a workaround, or tried anything DIY is signaling that the problem is in the "would be nice" tier — not the "I need this now" tier.
**How to apply:** Ask "What have you already tried?" and "How long have you been dealing with this?" If they've done nothing, probe on why. The answer usually reveals either that the pain is lower than they claimed, or that they don't believe a solution is possible — both of which are critical findings.
**Exception:** Problems people have normalized to the point of not realizing they're solvable (e.g., spreadsheet-based workflows that could be automated). In these cases, show them a better path exists before concluding they don't care.

### Rule: Extract specifics, not generalities
**Source:** The Mom Test — Rob Fitzpatrick
**The principle:** "Usually I..." or "Typically we..." is narrative, not data. "Last Tuesday I had to..." is signal. The move from the general to the specific is where the real insight lives.
**Why it works:** General answers are idealized reconstructions. Specific past events are actual memories — they include the real context, the exact tools used, the actual people involved, and the real emotional response. The specific story is where you discover the workarounds, the bottlenecks, and the moments of genuine frustration.
**How to apply:** Follow every general answer with "Can you take me to a specific time that happened?" or "Tell me about the most recent example." Stay in the specific story until you understand the full context: what triggered it, what they did, what broke down, what they wished had been different.
**Exception:** When you're doing pattern analysis across many interviews and need to see aggregate trends — generalities are useful for synthesis, not for individual interviews.

### Rule: Run customer touchpoints weekly, not quarterly
**Source:** Continuous Discovery Habits — Teresa Torres
**The principle:** Product teams make consequential decisions every single day. Any research cadence longer than a week creates a gap between the rate of decision-making and the rate of customer input.
**Why it works:** A team that interviews customers monthly makes roughly 20 working days of product decisions between each round of customer input. Those decisions compound on each other. By the time the next interview happens, the team has built significant momentum in a direction that may be disconnected from what customers actually need. Weekly interviews keep the feedback loop tight enough to catch wrong turns early.
**How to apply:** Block a standing weekly slot for customer conversations. The bar is low — even a 20-minute call counts. Use a rotating panel of participants or an always-on recruitment funnel so you never run out of people to talk to.
**Exception:** Products with genuinely difficult-to-access customer populations (e.g., senior executives, rare specialists). Even then, aim for biweekly and supplement with behavioral data to fill the gaps.

### Rule: Discovery is a team sport — PM, designer, and engineer together
**Source:** Continuous Discovery Habits — Teresa Torres
**The principle:** When research is outsourced to researchers who relay summaries back to the team, critical context and nuance gets lost in translation. The team that builds the product should hear customers directly.
**Why it works:** Engineers who hear customer frustrations firsthand make different technical trade-offs. Designers who observe actual workflows spot opportunities that no brief could capture. The interpretation that happens live in a conversation — noticing a hesitation, following an unexpected thread — cannot be faithfully transmitted in a slide deck. The team that does the research builds shared understanding that drives better decisions.
**How to apply:** Rotate the "lead" role in interviews across team members. The PM may lead the first few, but designers and engineers should run sessions too. Debrief immediately after each interview as a trio.
**Exception:** Specialized research that genuinely requires domain expertise (accessibility testing, clinical research, security audits). Even then, have at least one core team member observe each session.

### Rule: Map the full opportunity space before committing to any solution
**Source:** Continuous Discovery Habits — Teresa Torres
**The principle:** The first solution that surfaces is almost never the best one. Systematic mapping of the opportunity space prevents premature convergence on whatever idea came to mind first.
**Why it works:** Teams under delivery pressure jump to solutions because solutions feel like progress. But selecting a solution without understanding the full landscape of opportunities means potentially solving the least important problem, or solving a symptom rather than the root cause. Structured opportunity mapping — connecting business outcomes to customer needs to possible solutions — surfaces better options and creates defensible prioritization.
**How to apply:** Before generating solution ideas, map the opportunity space as a tree: start with the desired outcome, then enumerate the customer needs that relate to it, then identify sub-needs within each. Only when the opportunity map is visible should the team generate and evaluate solutions.
**Exception:** When operating in a well-understood domain with an existing, mature product — you may have already done enough opportunity mapping to move faster. But even then, revisit the map when tackling a genuinely new problem area.
