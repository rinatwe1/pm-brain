# Discovery Knowledge Base
## Pre-loaded Frameworks

---

### The Honest Interview Method — The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)
**What it is:** A discipline for conducting customer interviews that generates real signal instead of polite agreement. The central challenge of customer interviews is that people want to be encouraging and helpful — which means they'll validate almost anything you pitch. The solution is to restructure interviews so you're asking about facts and past behavior rather than about your idea.
**Core insight:** Any question that can be answered with "yes, I'd probably use that" is a bad question. The goal is to make the conversation so grounded in reality — their actual life, their actual problems, their actual behavior — that they can't give you a socially pleasing answer even if they want to.
**PM use case:** Before any customer development sprint, use this framework to audit your interview script. Kill every hypothetical. Replace every "would you" with "when did you last." Use it also in stakeholder interviews — the same principles apply when you're trying to understand what sales has actually promised customers vs. what they think customers want.

### Opportunity Solution Trees — Continuous Discovery Habits (Teresa Torres)
**What it is:** A visual structure that organizes the space between a business outcome and the solutions a team might build. The tree has four levels: desired outcome (top), opportunity layer (customer needs and pain points), solution layer (potential product responses), and assumption layer (what must be true for the solution to work).
**Core insight:** Most teams jump from "we need to improve retention" directly to "let's build X feature." The OST makes the intermediate layer — the opportunity space — explicit and visible. This exposes how many different needs could plausibly connect to the desired outcome, and surfaces the risk that the team has latched onto one opportunity prematurely without exploring the others.
**PM use case:** Use when kicking off a new product area, when a team is stuck debating between solutions, or when a roadmap feels unmoored from a clear business rationale. The tree makes the reasoning chain visible and debatable, which is much better than having it live implicitly in the PM's head.

### Jobs to Be Done — When Coffee and Kale Compete (Alan Klement)
**What it is:** A theory of customer motivation that reframes what customers are actually "buying." The claim is that people don't buy products — they bring products into their lives to make progress toward something they care about. The "job" is the unit of analysis: what progress is this person trying to make, in what context, against what alternatives?
**Core insight:** Competition is defined by the job, not by the product category. A customer deciding whether to use a product isn't comparing it to similar products — they're comparing it to every other way they could get that same job done. This reframe has radical implications for product design, positioning, and competitive analysis. It also explains why customers switch: not because a competitor is "better" in some abstract sense, but because the competitor gets their job done more completely or with less friction.
**PM use case:** Use JTBD when your product is losing to unexpected competitors, when you can't articulate what customers would miss if you disappeared, or when your user research produces feature requests rather than insight into underlying motivation. Switch interviews — talking to people who recently changed products — are the primary research method.

### The Four Forces of Switching — When Coffee and Kale Compete (Alan Klement)
**What it is:** A model that maps the four psychological forces that determine whether a customer switches from their current solution to a new one. Two forces push toward switching: the push of frustration with the current solution, and the pull of attraction toward the new one. Two forces resist switching: anxiety about change, and the habit of the current behavior.
**Core insight:** Most product teams design for pull — they make the new thing compelling — and almost entirely ignore anxiety and habit, which are often far more powerful. A product can be clearly better in every rational dimension and still fail to gain adoption because the switching cost (in mental effort, learning curve, workflow disruption, or social risk) exceeds the perceived benefit. Understanding which force is the real barrier changes the design priorities completely.
**PM use case:** Use when a product has good awareness and stated interest but low conversion or adoption. Map all four forces for the target user before assuming the problem is product quality. Often, reducing anxiety (better onboarding, reversible trials, social proof) or reducing habit friction (migration tools, familiar patterns) matters more than adding more pull.

### Value Proposition Canvas — Value Proposition Design (Osterwalder et al.)
**What it is:** A two-sided mapping tool that aligns what you build with what customers actually care about. One side maps the customer: what tasks they're trying to accomplish (jobs), what frustrates them in the process (pains), and what outcomes they'd like to achieve (gains). The other side maps the product: what it does (products and services), how it reduces friction (pain relievers), and how it creates positive outcomes (gain creators). Fit happens when the two sides align.
**Core insight:** The canvas makes misalignment visible before you've committed to building anything. Most product failures aren't execution failures — they're fit failures. The team built something that relieved pains customers didn't rank highly, or created gains customers didn't particularly want, while missing the one job that actually mattered. The canvas is a forcing function for this conversation.
**PM use case:** Use at the start of a product initiative to map your assumptions about the customer, and again after customer research to update the map with what you actually learned. Use it to evaluate solution ideas: does this pain reliever address a top-ranked pain, or a minor inconvenience?

### Continuous Discovery Cadence — Continuous Discovery Habits (Teresa Torres)
**What it is:** An operating model for product teams that treats customer research as an ongoing habit rather than a project-based activity. The minimum viable cadence is weekly customer touchpoints, conducted by the full product trio (PM, designer, engineer), in service of a specific desired outcome.
**Core insight:** The gap between research-project teams and continuous-discovery teams isn't just about frequency — it's about how research integrates with decisions. Research projects produce reports that sit between the research and the decisions. Continuous discovery means research directly informs the next experiment, the next interview, the next design iteration. The feedback loop is tight enough to actually change what the team does.
**PM use case:** Use this as the operating model baseline for any product team doing active development. The goal is to never go more than a week without talking to a customer. Set up an always-on recruitment mechanism (automated outreach, in-product recruitment prompts, ongoing customer panel) so the barrier to booking an interview is zero.
