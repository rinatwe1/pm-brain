# Discovery Hypotheses — Starting Points

## Common Hypotheses to Test
These are patterns observed across many products. Validate or invalidate for your specific context.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our current interview questions are generating social approval rather than honest signal
  Confidence: high (most teams start here)
  Source: Pattern from The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick) — hypothetical questions consistently produce inflated validation
  Test method: Audit your last interview guide. Flag every question that could be answered with "yes, that sounds useful." Replace each flagged question with a past-behavior equivalent. Run both versions with different participants and compare the quality of insight.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: The problem we're targeting is painful enough that customers have already taken action to address it
  Confidence: medium
  Source: Behavior-signal principle from The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick)
  Test method: In your next five interviews, ask "What have you already tried to solve this?" If more than half say "nothing yet," the problem urgency is lower than assumed. If they list multiple attempts, the problem is real.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: We have correctly identified the job our target customer is trying to get done, and our product is competing against the right alternatives
  Confidence: low (most teams under-scope their competitive frame)
  Source: Jobs to Be Done framework, When Coffee and Kale Compete (Klement)
  Test method: Run five switch interviews with customers who recently adopted your product or a competitor. Map what they were doing before, what triggered the search, what they considered, and what they ultimately chose. The competitive landscape that emerges is often different from what your product team assumed.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Switching anxiety and habit inertia are bigger barriers to adoption than product quality
  Confidence: medium (especially true for products displacing established workflows)
  Source: Four Forces of Progress model, When Coffee and Kale Compete (Klement)
  Test method: Interview five potential customers who were aware of your product but haven't adopted it. Ask what would need to change for them to switch. Categorize their answers: are they about product features (pull), or about risk, effort, and disruption (anxiety and habit)? The ratio determines where to focus.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our assumed customer pains and gains match what customers actually rank as most important
  Confidence: low (teams routinely overestimate the importance of easily-visible pains)
  Source: Value Proposition Canvas, Value Proposition Design (Osterwalder et al.)
  Test method: Write down your top five customer pains and top five customer gains. In your next round of interviews, ask customers to rank them in order of importance. Compare the rankings to your assumptions. Gaps reveal misalignment between what you're building relief for and what actually hurts.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our team is making product decisions that go more than a week without being validated by customer input
  Confidence: high (most teams are in monthly or quarterly research cycles)
  Source: Continuous discovery cadence principle, Continuous Discovery Habits (Torres)
  Test method: Audit the last six weeks of product decisions. For each significant decision (prioritization call, design choice, scope reduction), identify the most recent customer input that informed it. If any decision was made more than seven days after the most recent customer conversation, that decision was made in a vacuum.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our customer segment is more heterogeneous than our personas suggest, and insights from one sub-group don't generalize across the whole
  Confidence: medium (especially for products serving multiple company sizes or roles)
  Source: Segment variance observation, Continuous Discovery Habits (Torres)
  Test method: Stratify your next ten interviews by two or three segment variables (e.g., company size, role seniority, industry vertical). After each interview, tag the key insights to the segment. Look for patterns that appear in one sub-group but not others — these are signals that your product may need different positioning, onboarding, or features for different segments.
