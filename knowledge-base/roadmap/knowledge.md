# Roadmap Knowledge Base
## Pre-loaded Frameworks

---

### Goal-Oriented Roadmap (GO Roadmap) — Strategize (Roman Pichler)
**What it is:** A roadmap format organized by release goals rather than feature lists. Each release or time bucket has a stated goal — a desired outcome for customers or the business — followed by the features selected to achieve that goal. The features are secondary to the goal; the goal is the commitment.
**Core insight:** Most feature roadmaps communicate what will be built but not why. The GO format forces the "why" to be the primary unit — if you can't state the goal of a release in one sentence, you don't have a goal yet, and you shouldn't be populating the release with features. The goal also serves as a natural filter for scope debates: "does this feature serve the release goal?" resolves most arguments faster than any prioritization framework.
**PM use case:** Use when setting up or reformatting the team's roadmap for the first time. Use in quarterly planning sessions to align on goals before selecting features. Use in stakeholder reviews when the conversation devolves into feature debates — return to the goals layer and re-establish agreement there first.

### Outcome-Focused Roadmaps vs. Feature Lists — Escaping the Build Trap (Melissa Perri)
**What it is:** A distinction between two fundamentally different types of product roadmaps. A feature-focused roadmap is a list of things to build, organized by time. An outcome-focused roadmap is a list of changes to achieve for customers or the business, organized by priority. The former locks teams into solutions; the latter preserves the team's ability to discover better solutions.
**Core insight:** The pressure to produce feature-focused roadmaps almost always comes from outside the product team — from sales, from leadership, from customers who want to know exactly what they'll get and when. This pressure is understandable but dangerous: committing to a feature before doing discovery means committing to a solution before understanding the problem. The PM's job is to hold the line on outcome commitments while maintaining productive stakeholder relationships — which requires explicit language about why the distinction matters.
**PM use case:** Use as a framework for the quarterly roadmap conversation with leadership and sales. Present outcomes and their supporting rationale; defer the specific features to after discovery. When sales asks "but what exactly will be in Q3?", redirect to "here's what we're trying to achieve for customers in Q3, and we'll know the best way to achieve it after we run the discovery experiments in Q1."

### Release Goals as Decision Filters — Strategize (Roman Pichler)
**What it is:** The principle that each product release should have an explicit goal that functions as a filter for every feature debate during that release cycle. The release goal asks: what problem are we solving for which users with this release? Any proposed feature is evaluated against that question. If a feature doesn't serve the release goal, it belongs in a different release.
**Core insight:** Release goals are constraints — and the right constraints accelerate decision-making rather than slowing it down. When a team has no stated release goal, every feature debate is open-ended: anything could potentially be included. When a team has a clear release goal, most scope debates answer themselves. The goal transforms a political conversation ("whose priority wins?") into a principled conversation ("does this serve the goal?").
**PM use case:** Use at the beginning of any release planning session. State the goal before any feature is discussed. Force consensus on the goal before the feature list opens. Use the goal to close scope-creep conversations: "That's a good idea — it doesn't serve the goal of this release. Let's add it to the backlog for next quarter's goal-setting."

### The Alternative to Feature Roadmaps — INSPIRED (Marty Cagan)
**What it is:** A description of how the most effective product teams operate without traditional feature roadmaps. Instead of committing to a list of features by quarter, these teams operate with: a product vision that provides direction, OKRs that provide quarterly focus, and team autonomy to discover the best solutions within those constraints. The roadmap communicates priorities and direction — not a project plan.
**Core insight:** The two biggest problems with traditional feature roadmaps are: they imply commitment to solutions before discovery, and they create false urgency around specific features that sales and leadership then communicate to customers as promises. The teams that produce the best products are almost never the ones with the most detailed roadmaps — they're the ones with the clearest goals and the most effective discovery processes.
**PM use case:** Use to make the case for shifting the team's roadmap culture when the current roadmap is functioning as a feature commitment list rather than a strategic communication tool. The argument is not "we won't tell you what we're building" — it's "we'll tell you what we're trying to achieve and we'll be able to tell you exactly what we're building after we figure out the best way to achieve it."

### Portfolio Roadmaps — Strategize (Roman Pichler)
**What it is:** A higher-order roadmap that sits above individual product roadmaps and shows how each product or initiative contributes to the company's overall strategic goals. Rather than managing each product's roadmap in isolation, the portfolio roadmap makes resource allocation decisions, surfacing dependencies and conflicts between products that would otherwise be invisible until they become crises.
**Core insight:** Without a portfolio view, teams optimize locally — each product team pursues its own goals without visibility into what other teams are doing or how the collective portfolio serves the company strategy. Resource conflicts surface late, dependencies are discovered after commitments are made, and the aggregate portfolio may underperform even though each individual product is well-managed. The portfolio roadmap is the PM equivalent of the portfolio theory in investing: managing the whole is different from managing the parts.
**PM use case:** Use when managing multiple products simultaneously, or when operating in an organization where product teams have begun to develop in directions that aren't clearly connected to the same company strategy. Also use as input to headcount and budget conversations — the portfolio roadmap shows which products need more investment and why.
