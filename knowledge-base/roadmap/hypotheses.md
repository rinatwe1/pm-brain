# Roadmap Hypotheses — Starting Points

## Common Hypotheses to Test
These are patterns observed across many products. Validate or invalidate for your specific context.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Most items on our current roadmap cannot be traced to a clear strategic goal
  Confidence: high (the majority of roadmaps contain items that arrived through stakeholder requests or backlog inertia rather than strategic reasoning)
  Source: Strategic traceability principle, Strategize (Pichler); Escaping the Build Trap (Perri)
  Test method: Take each item on your current roadmap and apply the "five whys" upward: why are we building this? Why does that matter? Keep going until you either reach a clear business outcome or hit a dead end. Items that reach a dead end are not strategically grounded. Count how many items fail this test.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Stakeholders believe the roadmap represents commitments to specific features by specific dates, not a set of priorities subject to change
  Confidence: high (this misunderstanding is extremely common and produces organizational dysfunction)
  Source: Roadmap as communication tool, INSPIRED (Cagan)
  Test method: Ask three stakeholders from different functions (sales, leadership, customer success) what the roadmap "means." If any of them describe it as a commitment to deliver specific features — rather than a statement of priorities and intended outcomes — you have a roadmap culture problem. The fix requires explicit conversation about what the roadmap is and isn't, not just a format change.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our team is trying to run too many major initiatives simultaneously and making shallow progress on all of them
  Confidence: medium (common in teams with active stakeholders and broad product scope)
  Source: Focus concentration principle, Escaping the Build Trap (Perri)
  Test method: List every initiative your team is actively working on. For each, estimate the percentage of the team's capacity it's receiving. If you have five or more active initiatives and no single one is receiving more than 30% of team capacity, the roadmap is spread too thin. A quick gut-check: which initiative would you run at full team capacity if you could only do one? That answer reveals your real priority.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: No release in our current roadmap has an explicitly stated, testable goal
  Confidence: medium (many roadmaps have implied goals but rarely explicit, testable ones)
  Source: Release goal principle, Strategize (Pichler)
  Test method: Find your next planned release. Write its goal in one sentence — a specific outcome for specific users. If you can't, there's no goal yet. If you can write one, validate it with the team: do five people independently write the same goal? If not, there's no shared goal.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Sales or customer success have made specific feature commitments to customers based on the roadmap
  Confidence: high (this happens in most B2B organizations and is usually well-known but unaddressed)
  Source: Feature commitment risk, INSPIRED (Cagan); Escaping the Build Trap (Perri)
  Test method: Ask your sales or customer success team directly: "Have you committed any specific roadmap features to a customer as a reason for them to buy or stay?" If yes, name how many active commitments exist. This tells you the scope of the feature commitment debt and the urgency of shifting to outcome-based customer conversations.

---

- [ ] Hypothesis: Our roadmap has not been updated since it was created, making it a plan rather than a living tool
  Confidence: medium (roadmaps created in annual planning often calcify)
  Source: Living roadmap principle, Strategize (Pichler)
  Test method: Check the creation date and last-modified date of your current roadmap. If it hasn't changed significantly since it was created — even though experiments have been run, features have shipped, and market conditions have changed — it's a plan masquerading as a roadmap. The test of a healthy roadmap is that it has visible edit history showing substantive priority changes.
